Understanding Anxiety: What Your Mind and Body Are Telling You

By Dr. Vaishali Miglani, January 12, 2024

Anxiety is more than just feeling stressed or worried. While stress and anxious feelings are a common response to situations we perceive as difficult or threatening, anxiety is when these feelings don't go away and may occur without any particular reason or cause.

The Physical Manifestations of Anxiety

Your body has a built-in alarm system designed to protect you from danger. When you feel anxious, your body responds as if it's under attack, releasing a complex mix of hormones and chemicals like adrenaline, cortisol, and norepinephrine.

This fight-or-flight response can trigger physical symptoms including increased heart rate, sweating, trembling, dry mouth, chest pain, headaches, rapid breathing, and digestive issues. These symptoms are your body's way of preparing to deal with a perceived threat.

Cognitive Symptoms and How to Reframe Your Thoughts

Anxiety doesn't just affect your bodyâ€”it significantly impacts your mind as well. You might experience racing thoughts, difficulty concentrating, intrusive thoughts, or catastrophic thinking patterns where you automatically imagine the worst possible outcome.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) techniques can help reframe these thought patterns. The first step is recognizing when you're having anxious thoughts. Then, challenge these thoughts by asking yourself: "Is this thought realistic? What evidence do I have for and against this thought? What would I tell a friend who had this thought?"

Practical Strategies for Managing Anxiety

While professional help is important for chronic anxiety, there are several strategies you can use to manage symptoms:

  • Deep breathing exercises to activate the parasympathetic nervous system
  • Regular physical activity to reduce stress hormones
  • Mindfulness and meditation practices
  • Maintaining a consistent sleep schedule
  • Limiting caffeine and alcohol intake

Remember that anxiety is a common human experience, and seeking help is a sign of strength, not weakness. If your anxiety feels overwhelming or interferes with your daily life, consider reaching out to a mental health professional who can provide personalized support and treatment options.
